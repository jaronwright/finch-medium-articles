OpenAI Dropped GPT-5.5 the Friday Before the Musk Trial. That's Not a Coincidence.

GPT-5.5 launched this morning. DeepSeek V4 launched the same day. And on Monday, Elon Musk's lawsuit against Sam Altman goes to trial in Oakland.

OpenAI called GPT-5.5 their "smartest and most intuitive model yet" and the next step toward autonomous computer use. It excels at multi-step tasks, uses significantly fewer tokens in Codex, and ships with what they're calling their strongest safeguards to date. It rolls out to Plus, Pro, Business, and Enterprise tiers today.

The timing is not subtle. The Musk v. Altman trial starts Monday in Oakland. OpenAI wanted a headline before the courtroom circus begins. They got one.

DeepSeek V4 Dropped the Same Day

DeepSeek released V4 today claiming it matches leading models from Google, OpenAI, and Anthropic across the board. No pricing announced yet, but if the pattern holds, it'll undercut Western competitors by a significant margin.

Two major model releases on the same Friday. The coordination isn't coordination — it's just that this is what the AI industry looks like now. Multiple releases on the same day is no longer unusual. The pace of major drops has accelerated to the point where a single week can contain a new GPT, a new DeepSeek, Anthropic finding hundreds of Firefox bugs, and Google announcing its next-generation TPUs.

What GPT-5.5 Actually Is

"Smartest and most intuitive" is marketing. The specifics are more interesting: multi-step task performance, token efficiency in Codex, autonomous computer use progress.

The Codex token efficiency piece matters. If GPT-5.5 does the same work with fewer tokens, the economics of using it for coding agents change. That's a real competitive variable when you're running agents at scale, which more enterprises are every month.

The autonomous computer use angle is the longer game. OpenAI has been positioning Codex as a developer tool, but autonomous computer use is a different category — it's closer to what Anthropic's Claude Code does in agentic mode, or what the broader "agents operating independently" use case looks like in practice. This is the direction every frontier lab is moving.

The Week in Context

Let me zoom out for a second because the pace of this week is worth naming.

Google announced 75% of their code is now AI-generated. The number was 50% last fall. Anthropic's Mythos Preview found 271 Firefox bugs without a human in the loop. DeepSeek V4 dropped to compete with the best models in the world. GPT-5.5 launched hours before Musk's trial. And Sullivan and Cromwell — the white-shoe law firm handling Trump's cases — had to apologize to a federal judge for filing briefs full of AI-hallucinated citations.

That last one is worth a sentence. The law firm that supposedly knows better submitted fake case citations to a federal court. The legal industry's AI adoption is running ahead of its quality controls. That's a pattern that shows up in every industry that adopts a new tool fast: capability comes first, guardrails come second.

The Free Ride Ending

The Verge documented what's happening across AI platforms this week: ads, rate limits, feature restrictions, price hikes. The land-grab phase is over. The monetization phase has started.

I've been thinking about what this means for enterprise adoption. For companies that built workflows around generous free tiers, the shift to paid access changes the math. For companies that already paid, it changes less — but the rate structures are shifting too.

The transition from "AI as a product you try for free" to "AI as infrastructure you pay for" is the story of the next two years. Anthropic figured this out early with their API-first approach and their enterprise focus. The rest of the market is catching up.

The Inequality Problem Won't Go Away

MIT Nobel economist Daron Acemoglu published research this week arguing AI will widen inequality rather than democratize opportunity. His core argument: effective use of these tools requires education, abstract reasoning, and coding familiarity. The already-advantaged pull further ahead.

He's right about the near-term distribution. I've seen it in my own deployments — the users getting the most out of Claude at Capital One were already technically sophisticated. The non-technical use cases are growing but growing slower.

The tools are becoming more accessible. Whether they become accessible fast enough to change Acemoglu's prediction is the real question.
