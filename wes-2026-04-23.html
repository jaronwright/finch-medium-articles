Google Is Closing the Gap. Here's the One Number That Explains Why.

75% of all new Google code is now AI-generated and engineer-approved. Six months ago it was 50%. The direction of that trend tells you everything.

Google Cloud Next '26 was this week. The announcements ranged from interesting to important. But the number Sundar Pichai led with — 75% of new Google code generated by AI, up from 50% last fall — is the one worth sitting with.

That's not a demo. That's not an aspirational goal. That's the current state of engineering at one of the largest software companies on the planet. And it's moving fast.

The Hardware Story

Google announced two new TPU generations at Cloud Next. The TPU 8t (training) scales to 9,600 units with 2 petabytes of shared memory and 3x the processing power of the previous generation. The TPU 8i (inference) is purpose-built to run millions of agents concurrently at low latency.

That second one is the tell. You don't build hardware for millions of concurrent agents unless you're actually planning to run millions of concurrent agents. This isn't a capability demonstration. It's infrastructure investment for a specific future they're building toward.

Google also launched the Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform — described as mission control for companies managing thousands of AI agents rather than a handful. The scaling assumption is baked in.

Sergey Brin Was Right to Worry, But Google Is Moving

Earlier this week it came out that Sergey Brin sent a memo to DeepMind employees saying Google needed to catch up to Anthropic on AI coding agents, and that every Gemini engineer needed to be forced to use internal agents for complex, multi-step tasks.

At Cloud Next, Pichai confirmed that Google's internal agentic platform (called Antigravity) built the macOS Gemini app prototype in days, and completed a complex code migration six times faster than was possible a year ago.

So: Brin writes the alarm memo, Cloud Next shows the results. The gap between Google and Anthropic on coding agents may be narrower than the memo implied, or it may be closing faster than expected. Either way, the competitive dynamic is tighter than it looked two weeks ago.

Mythos Is Still the Story of the Week

The Google news doesn't change the Anthropic security story — it just shares the stage with it.

Claude Mythos Preview found thousands of high-severity vulnerabilities across every major operating system and web browser, fully autonomously. Mozilla's CTO confirmed 271 bugs in Firefox 150. Project Glasswing now includes Nvidia, Google, AWS, Apple, Microsoft, JPMorgan Chase, and about 40 others. Anthropic put $100 million in usage credits behind it.

The model is not publicly available because of the risks involved. That's an important sentence. This is Anthropic choosing not to release something they built because they think the security implications are significant enough to warrant keeping it controlled. The restraint is notable at a moment when the competitive pressure to ship is intense.

The Free Ride Is Over

The Verge documented the simultaneous wave of ads, rate limits, feature restrictions, and price hikes rolling through the industry. Every major AI lab is now pushing toward monetization because the math of free access at scale doesn't work.

This was always coming. The generous free tiers were a land-grab. Now the land has been grabbed and the question is who pays for the infrastructure.

I've been running Claude at enterprise scale for two years. We pay for it. We've always paid for it. The shift in the broader market toward paid access isn't surprising — it's just overdue.

The Inequality Problem

MIT Nobel laureate Daron Acemoglu published research today arguing AI will widen inequality rather than distribute opportunity. His framing: effective use requires substantial education, coding familiarity, and abstract thinking, so the gains flow to capital and skilled workers.

He's not wrong about the distribution. He may be underestimating the velocity of capability diffusion — tools that required technical depth a year ago are increasingly accessible — but the near-term pattern he describes is real. The people who benefit most from AI in 2026 are the people who were already positioned to benefit.

I think about this when I look at the 35,000 users we onboarded at Capital One. Most of them are technical. The non-technical use cases are growing, but they're growing slower. The gap between technical and non-technical utility is a real thing and the industry needs to be honest about it.

What This Week Added Up To

Google is moving faster than the Brin memo implied. Anthropic's Mythos is real and the results are quantified. The free tier era is ending. The inequality debate is arriving.

None of this is a surprise if you've been watching the space closely. All of it is worth writing down.
