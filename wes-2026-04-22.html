Mythos Found 271 Firefox Bugs Without a Human in the Loop

Mozilla's CTO confirmed it this morning. Anthropic's privately-held model is running vulnerability research at a speed and scale that didn't exist a month ago.

Bobby Holley, Mozilla CTO, confirmed today that Claude Mythos Preview found 271 bugs in Firefox 150. Entirely without human intervention. His exact framing: the model is "every bit as capable" as top security researchers.

The reassuring footnote he added: none of the bugs were beyond what an elite human could have caught. It's not outrunning the field. It's just moving faster and not stopping.

That distinction matters less than people think. The constraint on security research has never been capability. It's been time. Human researchers can only look at so much code in so many hours. A model that runs continuously, at the capability level of your best people, without sleeping, changes the math entirely.

271 bugs in Firefox alone. In a week.

Project Glasswing

Anthropic announced Project Glasswing alongside the Mozilla disclosure. It's a cybersecurity partnership that includes Apple, Nvidia, Google, Microsoft, Amazon, JPMorgan Chase, Cisco, CrowdStrike, and roughly 40 other organizations. Anthropic is putting $100 million in usage credits behind it.

The composition of that list is worth sitting with. These are not organizations that sign up for research previews. These are organizations with significant infrastructure and serious security requirements. They are running Mythos on real systems.

The model is not publicly available. It's operating privately, at this scale, right now.

The Pentagon Contradiction

Here's the interesting part: the NSA reportedly has access to Mythos even though the Department of Defense still officially classifies Anthropic as a supply chain risk.

Dario met with senior White House officials last week. Trump called Anthropic "very smart" and said a Pentagon deal is "possible." This followed the February standoff where Anthropic refused to allow its tech for mass domestic surveillance or fully autonomous lethal weapons — which is how they ended up on the supply chain risk list in the first place.

The government's relationship with Anthropic is now operating on multiple contradictory tracks simultaneously. The NSA is using the model. The DoD is officially skeptical. The White House is warming up. Anthropic hired a lobbying firm with direct Trump ties.

All of this is happening at once.

What Opus 4.7 Actually Is

In a notably honest system card, Anthropic acknowledged that Opus 4.7 — their most powerful publicly available model — does not advance the capability frontier because Mythos outscores it on every evaluation.

Opus 4.7 is still meaningfully better at complex software engineering, image analysis, and creative documents. Cursor, Replit, Notion, Shopify, and Vercel were early testers. Pricing is $5 per million input tokens, $25 per million output.

But the honest read is that Anthropic has two tiers right now: what you can buy, and what they're running privately. The gap between those tiers is significant and appears to be growing.

Sergey Brin Is Still Worried

A follow-up to the memo I wrote about yesterday: Brin's concern at Google is specifically about coding agents, not general AI capability. He wants every Gemini engineer forced to use internal agents for complex, multi-step tasks because he believes that's the prerequisite for building AI that can improve itself.

The framing reveals what the internal compass at Google looks like: they're behind on the thing that determines whether you can catch up. That's a specific kind of problem.
